Gen Z Expectations For The Workplace
Gen Z Isn’t Settling — They’re Redefining Work

By: Raisa Hoque


For Gen Z, work is more than a paycheck. They're looking for a purpose, balance, and a job that fits their needs, not the other way around. Generation Z, born roughly between 1997 and 2012, is now entering the workforce in full force. This generation came of age during a time defined by constant financial, political, and economic upheaval in the United States. Many watched their families struggle through the 2008 financial crisis, grew up under the shadow of rising student debt, and entered adulthood just as the COVID-19 pandemic upended traditional work and life expectations.

As a result, Gen Z workers are fundamentally reshaping what work should look like. They want more than just a paycheck: they seek jobs that align with their values, offer flexibility, protect their mental health, and contribute to a larger mission they believe in. Unlike previous generations, Gen Z expects their work to adapt to them and is unwilling to bend their lives to fit outdated corporate norms. Employers that fail to offer purpose, growth, and real work-life balance risk falling behind in a competitive job where Gen Z workers have more choices and higher expectations than ever before.

So what exactly does Gen Z want? According to Handshake, flexible schedules and strong mental health benefits top their list. For this generation, flexibility isn’t just about remote work: it’s about having real control over when and how they work so they can manage stress, protect their mental health, and make time for priorities outside the office. Many want the freedom to design work around life — whether that means four-day workweeks, adjustable hours, or hybrid options that respect their time and well-being.

As Nur, an Opportunity Network Fellow and a recent college graduate, explained, “I want to work hard, but I also want time for friends, family, and my own hobbies. Balance keeps me motivated.” Flexible schedules are just the start, Gen Z also expects employers to take mental health seriously. Knowing I can take a mental health day or get support if I need it makes me feel like the company actually cares,” Nur added, a view supported by research from Handshake and Deloitte, which found that burnout and stress are top concerns for this generation.

Handshake’s survey also shows Gen Z expects their employers to acknowledge and address urgent social issues, from racial justice and gender equality to climate action and community well-being. They’re more likely to choose and stay with companies that take public stands on causes that align with their values, instead of staying silent. As Nur shared, “It matters to see people with different backgrounds at all levels. It makes the workplace feel more welcoming and fair.”

But flexibility, well-being, and social impact are only part of the picture. Research from Stanford and EY highlights other top priorities: clear communication, meaningful feedback, and authentic leadership. Gen Z wants managers who check in often and invest in their growth, not just once a year. They also want freedom to pursue side projects, travel, and build a career that fits into a life. Good leadership and clear growth paths. That’s what would make me stay long-term,” Nur said, echoing what many surveys confirm. Gen Z wants to see where they’re going, and how they’ll get there.

Yet while Gen Z knows what they want, the traditional advice they get in college or from older generations doesn’t always match. As Nur noted, “Professors and career offices give helpful advice, but sometimes it feels a bit out of touch.” That’s exactly where programs like The Opportunity Network (OppNet) step in to close the gap. OppNet understands that young people need more than just generic career advice, they need real-world guidance that reflects what today’s workplaces actually demand. Through one-on-one coaching, mock interviews, networking opportunities, and practical resources like curated job boards, OppNet helps Gen Z build the skills and confidence to find roles that offer flexibility, strong mental health support, and inclusive cultures. Fellows like Nur benefit from a community of peers and mentors who share insights about negotiating for better conditions, asking the right questions during interviews, and advocating for growth opportunities once on the job. By combining career prep with a deep focus on equity, access, and personal development, OppNet equips Gen Z with the tools they need to shape their own careers — and push workplaces to meet them where they are.

At the end of the day, Gen Z is asking for more than trendy perks. They’re looking for workplaces that respect their lives, support their well-being, and stand for the values they believe in. Employers and educators who listen and adapt will be the ones who don’t just attract Gen Z — they’ll keep them motivated, loyal, and ready to lead the future of work.

Why Does Gen Z Want To Redefine The Workforce?

By: Ina Chen


Why Does Gen Z Want To Redefine The Workforce?

While commuting to an event one day, I overheard two people venting their frustrations. Both sighed about wanting to work comfortably in their own home. One of them said, “I'm just counting down to Friday so I can have the weekend to myself.” Later that same day, I attended an A+E Networks media event and witnessed a surprising scene. Three employees playing ping pong with one another. Though they kept missing the ball, the trio were having a blast with one another. Witnessing these events on the same day made me realize something. Gen Z wants to be in jobs where they can feel liberation.


Gen Z's demand for work-life balance

For Gen Z, flexibility isn't a perk, it's a priority for them. We've seen what the lack of flexibility does to people. From people ranting on TikTok or even talking about it in their actual workplace. Some even have emotional meltdowns online. In a 2024 study conducted by LinkedIn, 73% of Gen Z have left or considered leaving a job that didn't have flexible policies. A 2024 Deloitte report adds that 63% prefer hybrid work for better work-life balance. When it comes to time, Gen Z are drawing boundaries. To them their time is worth more than gold, something they can't get back. They see this as a necessary step to maintain their mental health and productivity. It's a plea to avoid the cycles of exhaustion that previous generations endured and viewed as normal.


Purpose Versus Paycheck

People from older generations think of jobs as climbing up the corporate ladder. However, the younger audience has a different take. They tie their work to purpose instead of a paycheck. According to a study conducted by Deloitte Global, 86% of Gen Zers want a sense of purpose in their work. Shockingly, in a Pew Research Study, 70% say that they are willing to be paid less to work for organizations whose values align with theirs. These values include things like environmental impact, diversity (DEI), mental health advocacy, and more. 44% mentioned how they were willing to reject a job offer if the company's ideals clash with theirs. It's evident that there's a shift from wanting just a paycheck to instead wanting a purpose and change. Gen Z views themselves as stakeholders rather than just workers.


The Shift In Workplace Culture

To older generations, asking for a nap room, ping-pong, or even pet friendly environments at work sounds frivolous. To Gen Z these “frivolous” ideas are a symbol of respect. At the A+E media event, I saw firsthand how a joyful environment promotes both productivity and collaboration.

The truth of the matter is this generation values mental health, flexibility, and community just as much as salary and benefits, sometimes even more. In a New York Post survey, nearly 1 in 6 Gen-Zers won't consider a job without a designated nap space at their workplace. 1 in 5 Gen Z workers say having a "fun room" with games is essential. 33% of Gen Z expect free snacks and lunches at work, more than any other group.

Even with perks companies may offer, many still resist returning to traditional office settings. 1 in 4 people want extended sabbaticals for personal development or even travel. 20% won't consider a job unless the office allows pets. These "frivolous" ideas allow people to integrate work, wellness, and passion together. In fact, at the A+E media event, Paul Bucceri talked about how he didn't believe in work-life balance. He instead, found ways to integrate his life with work. He once took his daughters out of school for a road trip while working.


Let People Breathe

Overall, Gen Z's expectations at work may seem to be idealistic. However, they have experienced the pandemic, social justice issues, burnout and other issues. This isn't about finding ways to do less work, rather it is about finding a system that nurtures their productivity. If employers decide to ignore these pleas they risk missing out on a generation who are socially aware and mentally conscious. Instead, let's stop trying to cling onto outdated norms and start building a workforce based on new innovative ideas.

Gen Z in the Workplace

By: Karlens Viltus


As Generation Z steps into the workforce, they’re bringing a new set of expectations that are shifting the culture of employment. Unlike previous generations who prioritized job security, longevity, and climbing the corporate ladder, Gen Z is focused on purpose, flexibility, mental health, inclusion, transparency, and growth. These priorities aren’t based on entitlement, but rather on the social, economic, and technological environment they grew up in. This generation has come of age in a world marked by climate change, racial justice movements, student debt, the COVID-19 pandemic, and the rise of artificial intelligence. As a result, they have redefined what a fulfilling and sustainable career looks like.

One of the strongest expectations Gen Z holds is that work must carry meaning. According to Deloitte’s 2024 Global Gen Z and Millennial Survey, 86% of Gen Z respondents said that having a sense of purpose is a key factor when choosing an employer (Deloitte, 2024). Gen Z wants to work for companies that do more than generate profit — they want to contribute to something that matters. One student I interviewed shared, “I need to feel like what I’m doing actually matters. I don’t want to work somewhere that just cares about profit.” This sentiment reflects a broader desire among young professionals to align their careers with their personal values, whether that’s sustainability, equity, or community impact. When companies fall short of this alignment, Gen Z isn’t afraid to disengage or walk away.

Flexibility is another non-negotiable. The pandemic showed this generation that traditional office models aren’t the only way to be productive. As a result, remote and hybrid work structures are now seen as standard. A report by McKinsey & Company found that 77% of Gen Z workers prefer hybrid or remote arrangements and consider flexibility a critical component of job satisfaction (McKinsey & Company, 2022). Another interviewee stated, “I want flexibility, like the option to work from home at least part of the week. I don’t see myself doing a strict 9 to 5 every day in an office.” For Gen Z, flexible work signals trust and autonomy. It allows them to structure their days in ways that support both their personal lives and productivity, which can, in turn, build loyalty to the employer.

In addition to flexibility, mental health support is a major priority. The American Psychological Association reported in 2023 that Gen Z experiences higher levels of stress than any other generation, largely due to work-related anxiety and financial uncertainty (APA, 2023). As a result, this generation expects mental health resources to be built into their benefits. One respondent emphasized this need, saying, “For me, mental health benefits are just as important as the salary. If I’m burnt out, I won’t be productive anyway.” Employers that offer counseling, mental health days, and emotionally intelligent leadership are more likely to retain Gen Z workers, while those who fail to support emotional well-being risk high turnover and disengagement.

Diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) are also top concerns. A 2023 survey by Monster found that 83% of Gen Z candidates consider a company’s commitment to DEI as a major factor in accepting a job offer (Monster, 2023). Gen Z expects meaningful representation at all levels of leadership, inclusive hiring practices, and safe environments for people of all identities. But they also demand authenticity. If a company makes surface-level DEI claims without action, they’re likely to be called out — often publicly. Gen Z is unafraid to use social media as a tool for accountability, and performative behavior is quickly exposed.

Professional development is another key expectation. Gen Z doesn’t want to be stuck in repetitive roles with no clear path forward. Instead, they want consistent feedback, mentorship, and access to learning opportunities. According to LinkedIn’s Workforce Confidence Index, 76% of Gen Z professionals believe that ongoing learning is essential to career success (LinkedIn, 2022). One peer I spoke to said, “I expect regular feedback and chances to grow. If I’m stuck doing the same thing for a year, I’m gone.” This generation views skill-building not just as a career benefit, but as a necessity for survival in an ever-changing job market. Transparency and communication from leadership also matter. Gen Z wants honesty about company performance, expectations, and decisions that impact their roles. PwC’s 2023 Future of Work report found that Gen Z employees value open conversations with managers, including regular feedback and clarity on goals (PwC, 2023). Given that they’ve grown up with constant access to information online, they expect the same openness within their workplaces. Companies that lack transparency risk damaging the trust Gen Z considers foundational.

Finally, Gen Z’s expectations are being shaped by the growing influence of artificial intelligence on hiring and job security. Several of the students I interviewed expressed frustration about AI’s role in the application process. One shared, “It’s crazy. Entry-level jobs now require three years of experience, or worse, they’re just being automated.” Another said, “I’ve applied to over 50 jobs and barely heard back. Sometimes I wonder if a bot even looked at my résumé.” These quotes reveal a sense of discouragement and anxiety, particularly for recent graduates who feel overshadowed by algorithms and automation. “It’s like I studied for four years just to lose out to a robot in the application process,” one student said. Another added, “I feel like AI is moving faster than schools can teach us. We’re graduating behind.”

In conclusion, Gen Z’s workplace expectations reflect a shift toward a more human-centered, value-driven, and transparent approach to employment. They want flexibility, purpose, mental health support, DEI, growth, and honesty. These are not demands for special treatment, but rather a reflection of how the world has changed and what a sustainable, fulfilling career now looks like. Employers who adapt to these expectations will not only attract top Gen Z talent but will build stronger, more resilient organizations for the future.
